Fire pit questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best fire pit for you depends on your yard size, fuel preference, and how much maintenance you want. Wood pits offer a classic experience but require ash cleanup and safety precautions, while gas models light instantly and burn cleanly. This guide covers the key questions to help you choose wisely.

What type of fire pit is best for my yard?

Start by looking at your available space and local rules. A small, portable wood-burning bowl works well for a cozy backyard or campsite, while a larger permanent stone pit suits bigger gatherings. If you have a patio or deck, a propane fire pit table is often the most practical choice, it raises the flame off the ground and keeps smoke away from seated guests. For apartment balconies or very small yards, gel-fuel or tabletop alcohol fire pits are the safest bet, though they put out less heat. Always check your HOA or municipal codes before buying; many areas restrict open flames during dry seasons or require a minimum distance from structures.

Wood-burning vs gas: which is easier to maintain?

Wood-burning fire pits demand the most upkeep. You need to clean out ash after each use, store dry wood, and regularly inspect the bowl or ring for rust. Over time, soot stains the surrounding stone or metal. Many owners say the extra work is worth it for the smell and sound of crackling wood. Gas fire pits, propane or natural gas, are nearly maintenance-free. There is no ash, no sparks, and no smoke sticking to your clothes. You just turn a knob to light it and turn it off when you are done. The trade-off is a higher upfront cost and the need to replace propane tanks or arrange a gas line hookup. Experts note that gas models also produce less radiant heat than a comparable wood fire.

How do I choose a safe location for a fire pit?

Safety starts with placement. Fire pits should sit on a non-combustible surface like concrete, brick, gravel, or stone pavers, never directly on grass or wood decking (unless the pit is designed for decks and you use a heat shield mat). Keep the pit at least 10 feet from your house, fences, sheds, trees, and overhanging branches. Wind direction matters too. Position the pit so prevailing winds blow smoke away from seating areas and neighbors. Many communities require a 3-foot clearance around the pit with no flammable furniture or plants. A spark screen or mesh lid is essential for wood fires to catch embers, and a fire extinguisher or garden hose should always be within arm’s reach.

What materials should I look for in a fire pit?

Steel is the most common material because it holds heat well and is affordable. Look for heavy-gauge steel (at least 1/8 inch thick) that is powder-coated or painted with high-temp paint to resist rust. Cast iron retains heat beautifully and can last decades, but it is very heavy and prone to cracking if water gets trapped and freezes. For permanent pits, stone, brick, or concrete block offer durability and a classic look. However, they crack over time from repeated heating and cooling. Stainless steel is the premium choice for gas models, it resists corrosion and looks modern, but it conducts heat away from the fire, so the fire itself may feel slightly less warm. Avoid thin, cheap metal bowls unless you plan to use the pit only occasionally.

Do I need accessories like a spark screen or cover?

A spark screen is not optional for wood-burning pits, it is a safety necessity. Embers can pop out and ignite dry grass, deck boards, or clothing. Look for screens with a fine mesh (about 1/4 inch or smaller) that sit snugly over the opening. Some pits come with one included; if not, budget for a separate purchase. A weather cover also pays for itself. Rain and snow cause rust and cracking, especially in steel and cast iron models. Covers with a UV-resistant, waterproof fabric and drawstring closure fit most standard pits. For gas fire pits, a dedicated cover protects the burner assembly and logs from debris. Many owners recommend storing the propane tank separately when the pit is not in use.

How much does a good fire pit cost (in general terms)?

Fire pit prices span a wide range based on size, material, and fuel type. Small portable wood-burning bowls are very budget-friendly, often costing under a hundred dollars. Mid-range steel or cast-iron pits with a cooking grate and spark screen run a few hundred dollars, which is where most buyers find the best value. Propane fire pit tables and gas burning units typically cost several hundred to over a thousand dollars, especially if they include a lava rock bed or decorative glass. Natural gas conversion kits add to the investment. Permanent stone or masonry pits built into a patio can run into the thousands. In every category, you get what you pay for in terms of durability and safety features.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a fire pit on a wooden deck?

Yes, but only with precautions: place a non-combustible heat shield mat under the pit, keep it at least 10 feet from the house, and always use a spark screen. Propane fire pits are generally safer on decks because they produce no embers. Check your deck weight limit before placing a heavy stone pit.

Do I need a permit for a fire pit?

Many cities and counties require a permit for permanent fire pits over a certain size, and some ban open wood fires entirely during dry seasons. Portable propane pits often need no permit, but your HOA may have its own rules. Always call your local fire department or building department before buying.

How do I clean a fire pit?

For wood pits, let ash cool fully, then scoop it out and dispose of it in a metal container. Wipe down the bowl with a dry cloth. For gas pits, brush debris off the burner, check the gas ports for blockage, and clean the lava rocks or glass occasionally with warm water and a mild soap.

What size fire pit do I need?

A diameter of 24 to 30 inches works well for a couple or small family. For groups of 6 to 8, look for 36 to 48 inches. Remember that the pit size affects heat output and how far guests need to sit from the flames, too large can be uncomfortably hot for close seating.

Are propane fire pits safer than wood-burning ones?

Generally, yes, they produce no sparks, less smoke, and you can turn them off instantly. However, propane tanks can leak or be damaged, so store and connect them carefully. Wood fires require constant supervision but are safe when you follow basic rules: keep a screen in place and never leave unattended.

Can I cook on a fire pit?

Many wood-burning pits include a cooking grate for roasting marshmallows or grilling hot dogs. Some have adjustable grills for higher-heat cooking. Gas fire pits are not designed for cooking because the flame is decorative and uneven. If cooking is a priority, look for a dedicated fire pit with a purpose-built grate.

In shortA fire pit is a wonderful addition to your outdoor space when you match it to your lifestyle and follow basic safety rules. Take the time to research your local regulations, choose a fuel type that fits your maintenance tolerance, and invest in a durable model with the right accessories. The coziest fires are the ones you can enjoy worry-free for years to come.
